How should you deliver your project?

Different projects need different approaches — there is no one-size-fits-all. Choosing the right method can save time, money, and a great deal of frustration.

Structure Planned stages, predictable output.
Flexibility Responding to change, learning as you go.
Fit The method serves the project — not the other way round.
Why it matters

The way you manage a project shapes the outcome.

Picking the wrong method won't just make a project harder — it can quietly sabotage it. A rigid plan stops a learning project adapting. A loose approach lets a well-defined project drift into delays.

Different projects call for different things:

  • Some projects need structure and control — where the goal is clear and the path is known
  • Others need flexibility and adaptation — where you're learning as you build

Research insight

Different project methodologies suit different types of projects — performance depends on matching the approach to the nature of the work, rather than adopting a single method universally.

— Serrador & Pinto (2015), International Journal of Project Management
The three approaches

Three ways to run a project.

Almost every project methodology is a variant of one of these three patterns. Understand them well and you can run — or advise on — practically anything.

Method 01

Waterfall

Step by step.

A structured, linear approach where each phase is completed in full before the next begins. You plan everything upfront, then execute the plan.

Characteristics

  • Clear, sequential stages
  • Fixed plan and scope
  • Predictable and measurable

Best for

  • Simple, well-understood projects
  • Fixed requirements that won't shift
  • Clear, externally-imposed deadlines
Example

Building a simple brochure website where the content, design, and launch date are agreed upfront.

Method 02

Agile

Flexible & iterative.

Work is broken into small pieces, delivered in short cycles, with regular feedback driving the next round. You adjust continuously as you learn.

Characteristics

  • Short cycles (usually 1–4 weeks)
  • Adaptive — the plan evolves
  • Regular feedback and review

Best for

  • Projects with changing requirements
  • Digital products and services
  • Small, collaborative teams
Example

Developing a donor-facing app where the features evolve based on how early users actually behave.

Method 03

Hybrid

The best of both.

Use structure where it helps, flexibility where you need it. Most real-world projects in small organisations end up here, whether they're labelled that way or not.

Characteristics

  • Fixed overall timeline
  • Flexible delivery within phases
  • Balances planning with learning

Best for

  • Most real-world projects
  • Teams new to project management
  • Small organisations with mixed needs
Example

Planning an annual fundraising campaign to a fixed launch date, but iterating the content and channels as you learn what's working.

At a glance

A quick side-by-side comparison.

The three methods mapped against the four factors most small organisations care about.

Approach Structure Flexibility Best for
Waterfall High Low Fixed-scope, well-understood projects
Agile Low High Changing or experimental projects
Hybrid Medium Medium Most real-world projects
Decision

Choose based on your situation — not the trend.

Ignore what's fashionable in tech right now. Start with a single question: how well do you understand the scope before you begin?

If your scope is Fixed & clear Waterfall
If your scope is Uncertain or changing Agile
If your scope is A mix of both Hybrid

A useful rule of thumb

If you can write a detailed, accurate plan before starting — lean Waterfall. If you'd be guessing at most of the plan — lean Agile. If you're somewhere in between (you usually are), plan the skeleton and iterate the flesh. That's Hybrid.

Implementation

Turning plans into action.

The plan tells you what should happen. Implementation is what actually happens. The gap between the two is where most projects struggle — not because the plan was wrong, but because the work of doing the work is harder than the work of planning it.

From plan to action

Treat the first week as a deliberate handover from planning mode into doing mode. Confirm who's owning what. Make sure the first task is small enough that it actually gets started.

Track progress regularly

A 15-minute weekly check-in beats a two-hour monthly review. You catch issues while they're small, and people stay accountable to their own commitments.

Manage issues and changes

Things will change. Decide upfront how you'll handle changes — log them, decide whether to absorb or push back, communicate the impact. Don't let them creep in silently.

Adapt when needed

If reality and the plan disagree, the plan is wrong. Update it. A plan you stop trusting is a plan no one follows.

Four habits that make implementation work

  • Start with small tasks. The first action should take less than two hours. Momentum is built by finishing things — not by planning bigger ones.
  • Track progress regularly. Pick a rhythm — weekly check-in, fortnightly review — and stick to it. The cadence matters more than the format.
  • Communicate frequently. Short updates beat long reports. A two-line message every Friday tells your team and stakeholders more than a monthly deck.
  • Adjust the plan when needed. If a deadline slips or a task changes shape, update the plan that day. Don't let the document drift away from reality — it loses authority fast.

In practice: a small charity launching a new newsletter

The plan: launch a monthly email newsletter to 500 supporters within 6 weeks. Week 1 (start small): set up the email tool and import the contact list — that's it. Weeks 2–3 (track progress): draft and review issue 1 in a 30-minute Tuesday call each week. Week 4 (adapt): the designer gets sick — switch to a simpler text-first format rather than waiting. Week 5 (communicate): send a short message to the board on Friday saying "we're on track, format change noted." Week 6: launch. The plan didn't survive contact with reality unchanged — but the project did, because the team adapted instead of insisting on the original design.

Key takeaway

Implementation is mostly about rhythm and adaptation. Start small, check in regularly, communicate often, and update the plan when reality changes. The team that adjusts beats the team that insists.

In practice

What works best for small organisations?

Big project management frameworks were designed for big projects and big teams. For a small business or a small charity, a lighter, more adaptive approach almost always wins in practice.

Reality 01

Small teams

With fewer people, communication is simpler. Agile works naturally — a weekly check-in replaces layers of meetings and reports.

Reality 02

Limited resources

You can't afford certified consultants or complex tooling. Keep the method simple — Trello or a shared doc is genuinely enough for most projects.

Reality 03

Shifting conditions

Funding rounds, volunteer availability, and external demands change mid-project. Some flexibility isn't optional — it's survival.

For most Irish small businesses and charities, the honest answer is Hybrid — a fixed overall timeline that your funders or board expect, with enough flexibility inside it to adapt as you learn.

Avoid this

The three mistakes people make when choosing.

Less about the methods themselves and more about how people approach the choice.

Choosing a method because it's fashionable, without actually understanding how it works in practice.
Over-engineering the process — adding frameworks, rituals, and tools that don't fit the project's size.
Following a methodology too strictly, even when the project is clearly asking for something different.
Key takeaway
The best method is the one that fits your project — not the most popular one, and not the one your last job used.
