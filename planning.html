Topic 03

Plan your project properly — or plan to struggle.

A simple, practical approach to planning projects that actually get finished. No complex tools, no overthinking — just clear steps that work.

Focus A clear goal everyone can repeat from memory.
Structure Work broken into pieces small enough to do.
Ownership Every task has a name next to it — not a team.
Why it matters

Projects rarely fail because of bad ideas.

They fail earlier — in the quiet work that should have happened before anyone started building. Most project disasters trace back to a planning gap, not an execution mistake.

The recurring planning failures that sink small-organisation projects:

  • No clear plan — just a shared sense of "we should probably do this"
  • Unclear roles — everyone assumes someone else is handling it
  • Unrealistic timelines — wishful thinking disguised as a schedule
  • Poor communication — decisions made in side conversations, never written down

Evidence base

Structured planning remains one of the strongest predictors of project success — more so than team size, budget, or methodology choice.

— Project Management Institute (2021), PMBOK Guide
The basics

A good plan answers five simple questions.

If you can confidently answer these five before you start, you have a plan. If you can't, you have an intention — which is not the same thing.

01

Goal

What are we trying to achieve?

02

Scope

What's included — and what isn't?

03

Timeline

When will it happen — and by when?

04

People

Who is doing the work?

05

Budget

What will it cost — money, time, effort?

How to plan

Four steps. Each one concrete.

You don't need a software tool for this. You don't need a methodology certification. You need these four steps, done honestly.

01

Define the goal

Write it in one sentence a stranger could understand.

  • Be specific, not aspirational
  • Include a number or date where possible
  • Agree it before starting
02

Break the work down

List the tasks needed to get there. Keep it simple.

  • One action per task
  • Small enough to finish in a day or two
  • Don't over-engineer the list
03

Set a timeline

Add dates. Include milestones so you can check progress.

  • Start and end dates for every task
  • Identify a few key milestones
  • Build in buffer — you will need it
04

Assign responsibility

Every task gets a person's name. Not a team — a person.

  • One owner per task
  • The owner doesn't have to do all the work
  • But they're accountable for its completion

Planning pulls everything else together

A good plan isn't a standalone document — it draws on every other topic in this guide. Use Strategy to test whether the goal is worth pursuing, People to map stakeholders and decide owners, Risk & Governance to surface what could go wrong, and Delivery to pick the right approach for how the work will actually happen.

In practice

A worked example: a small charity website launch.

Here's what a lightweight plan looks like in real life — for a small Irish community charity launching a basic site with a donations page.

Element Detail
Goal Launch a simple website that lets visitors donate online — aiming for 30+ online donations in the first month.
Scope Homepage, about page, contact form, donation page with Stripe. No blog, no member login — those come later.
Timeline 4 weeks end-to-end. Week 1: content. Weeks 2–3: design & build. Week 4: testing & launch.
Team 1 volunteer content lead (5 hrs/week) · 1 freelance designer (paid, 20 hrs total) · 1 board member to approve.
Budget €200 total — designer fee, domain, and first year of hosting. No other hard costs.
Main risk Designer unavailable — mitigation: scope kept small enough to build with a template if needed.

What makes this plan work

Nothing fancy — no Gantt charts, no kickoff document. But every row is specific, measurable, and owned. That's the difference between a plan and a wish.

Tools

You don't need complex software.

The tool doesn't make the plan. A shared document that people actually read beats any piece of project management software no-one opens. Three options cover most small-org needs.

Trello
Break work into visual task cards on a shared board. Best for: projects where "what's next" matters more than "when exactly."
Notion
Plan, document, and collaborate in one shared space. Best for: teams that want their plan and their working notes together.
Microsoft Excel
Timeline, budget, and task tracking in a tool everyone already has. Best for: straightforward projects where simple wins.
Avoid this

Four planning failures that keep coming back.

Every one of these is easy to spot once you know what to look for — and each has a simple fix.

Planning too much — the plan becomes the project, and the real work keeps getting postponed.
Not planning at all — "we'll figure it out as we go" is a fast route to a predictable mess.
Unrealistic timelines — built on best-case assumptions, with no allowance for the inevitable problems.
No clear ownership — tasks assigned to "the team" or "we", which in practice means no-one.
Key takeaway
A simple plan you actually use beats a perfect plan you ignore — every single time.
