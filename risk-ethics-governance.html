Topic 05

Managing risk, ethics & governance.

Projects don't fail only because of poor planning. They fail because risks are ignored, decisions are unclear, or ethical issues are overlooked. For small businesses and charities, managing this well protects three things that really matter.

Time & budget Avoid overruns that a small organisation can't absorb.
Reputation Built slowly, lost quickly — especially for charities.
Stakeholders The funders, staff, volunteers, and beneficiaries you serve.
The basics

What is risk in a project?

A risk is anything that could affect your project — positively or negatively. Most people only think about negative risks, but upside risks (unexpected opportunities) matter too.

Here are five common types of project risk you'll encounter in a small organisation:

Delays Milestones slipping beyond deadline.
Budget overrun Costs rising beyond available funds.
Resource gaps Missing skills, tools, or capacity.
Technical issues Systems, software, or data failures.
Stakeholder conflict Disagreement on priorities or scope.
Why it matters

Risk management isn't bureaucracy. It's foresight.

Structured risk management gives you a reliable way to spot problems before they bite, make clearer decisions, and improve your chances of actually finishing the project.

Done well, risk management helps you:

  • Anticipate problems before they happen
  • Reduce uncertainty and nasty surprises
  • Make decisions with better information
  • Improve the likelihood of project success

Research insight

Structured risk management significantly improves project outcomes, including cost, schedule, and stakeholder satisfaction.

— Project Management Institute (2021)

Key takeaway

Risk management isn't about being pessimistic — it's about being prepared. Twenty minutes of honest "what could go wrong?" thinking at kickoff saves weeks of firefighting later.

See the four-step process
The process

Four steps. That's it.

Good risk management is a cycle, not a one-off exercise. Work through these four steps at kickoff, then repeat them at every milestone.

01

Identify

Ask: what could go wrong? And what unexpected good could happen?

  • Brainstorm with the team
  • Check past projects
  • Talk to stakeholders
02

Assess

For each risk, judge two things as honestly as you can.

  • How likely is it?
  • How bad would the impact be?
  • Rank: low, medium, high
03

Plan responses

Pick one of four strategies for each risk.

  • Avoid — remove the cause
  • Reduce — lower likelihood or impact
  • Accept — live with it
  • Transfer — insure or outsource
04

Monitor

Review regularly. New risks emerge; old ones fade.

  • Check at every milestone
  • Update the risk register
  • Flag new risks early
Template

A simple risk register.

Keep yours in a spreadsheet. Four columns is enough — don't over-engineer it.

Risk Likelihood Impact Action
Delay in delivery Medium High Add buffer time to critical path
Budget increase Low High Monitor costs weekly; cap discretionary spend
Key staff unavailable High Medium Assign backup; document processes
Data breach or loss Low High Backup weekly; GDPR-compliant tools only
Stakeholder disengagement Medium Medium Fortnightly check-ins; clear updates

Common trap

A risk register that no-one looks at after week one is worse than no register at all — it creates the false confidence that risks are handled. Schedule a recurring 15-minute review, or it won't happen.

Ethics

Doing the right thing, especially when it's difficult.

Ethics in project management isn't abstract philosophy. It shows up in small, daily decisions about how you report progress, spend money, and treat people.

What ethical project practice looks like

  • Honesty in reporting progress — including when things are going badly.
  • Responsible use of funds — particularly where the money is public or donated.
  • Respecting stakeholders — their time, their opinions, and their right to be informed.
  • Protecting sensitive data — GDPR compliance isn't optional.

Why it matters

Unethical decisions — even small ones — lead to loss of trust, legal exposure, and reputational damage. For charities, this is existential: your work depends on public trust and donor confidence. One bad headline can undo years of good work.

Governance

Who makes decisions — and who's accountable for them?

Governance is just the structure that answers those two questions. Even small projects need it. Without it, decisions stall, responsibility blurs, and when something goes wrong, no-one owns the fix.

Three things make up the spine of good governance:

Defined roles Everyone knows what they own and what they don't.
Clear decision paths When a call needs making, you know who makes it.
Accountability Oversight happens at set intervals, not when it's too late.
Irish context

Governance in Ireland: the Charities Regulator.

If you're running an Irish registered charity, governance isn't just good practice — it's a regulatory requirement. The Charities Governance Code is built on four principles every registered charity must apply.

Applying these principles isn't just about staying compliant. It's how Irish charities demonstrate trustworthiness to funders like Pobal and to the donating public.

Principle 01

Leadership

The board provides strategic direction and sets the charity's values.

Principle 02

Control

Proper financial and operational oversight of the charity's activities.

Principle 03

Transparency

Open communication with members, donors, and beneficiaries.

Principle 04

Accountability

Responsibility for decisions, actions, and the use of resources.

Emerging risk

Using AI responsibly in projects.

AI is increasingly used for planning, content creation, and data analysis — including by the smallest organisations. The speed and cost savings are real. So are the risks, and for charities handling sensitive data, they're significant.

Benefits

  • Saves time on repetitive drafting and summarising
  • Improves efficiency for small teams with limited capacity
  • Supports decision-making with quick analysis of data
  • Helps generate first drafts you can refine

Risks

  • Inaccurate or fabricated information ("hallucinations")
  • Bias inherited from training data
  • Data privacy concerns when inputting sensitive information
  • Over-reliance on outputs without verification

EU AI Act — Regulatory context

The EU AI Act takes a risk-based approach to AI regulation. Higher-risk AI systems face stricter controls, and all organisations deploying AI must ensure transparency and accountability. For Irish small businesses and charities, this means being able to explain how and why AI is used in any decision that affects people.

Four rules for responsible AI use

  • Don't rely fully on AI decisions. Treat AI as a draft, not an answer. A human always signs off.
  • Verify outputs. Check facts, figures, and citations — AI confidently invents all three.
  • Avoid sharing sensitive data. Don't paste donor lists, beneficiary details, or confidential plans into public AI tools.
  • Be transparent. If AI meaningfully shaped a decision or output, say so.
Key takeaway

AI is a powerful support tool, not a decision-maker. Use it to save time on first drafts and analysis — but verify every output, protect sensitive data, and keep a human in the loop for anything that matters.

Practical summary

Do this. Avoid this.

Worth doing

  • Keep risk management simple but consistent
  • Document key decisions and who made them
  • Assign clear responsibility for every risk
  • Review risks at every milestone, not just at kickoff
  • Be open and transparent with stakeholders

Common mistakes

  • Ignoring risks until they become problems
  • Making decisions with no clear owner
  • Lack of accountability when things slip
  • Over-reliance on AI outputs without verification
  • Poor communication with the board or funders
Key takeaway
Managing risk, ethics, and governance isn't about complexity. It's about awareness, responsibility, and control — and a simple approach, done consistently, protects your organisation and the people it serves.
