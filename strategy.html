Topic 02

Start the right projects — not just more projects.

Before you plan anything, decide if the project is actually worth doing. Good project management starts with good decisions — not longer to-do lists.

Purpose Every project needs a clear reason to exist.
Alignment It must support your organisation's goals.
Priority Choosing what to do beats doing everything.
Why it matters

Not every idea should become a project.

Most projects that fail don't fail in execution. They fail in selection — the wrong project was picked in the first place, usually for emotional or political reasons rather than strategic ones.

The three most common reasons small-organisation projects drain effort without producing value:

  • They don't solve a real problem — just a perceived or political one
  • They aren't aligned with the organisation's actual goals
  • They waste resources that should be going elsewhere

Strategic alignment

Projects should align with organisational strategy to deliver real value — a project that succeeds against its own objectives but doesn't serve the organisation's strategy has failed the organisation.

— Kerzner (2017), Project Management: A Systems Approach
Core idea

A simple way to decide if a project is worth it.

A business case is the document (or sometimes just a one-page memo) that makes the case for doing a project. It's the evidence you bring to the decision.

At its simplest, a business case answers four questions:

  • Why are we doing this? What problem or opportunity are we responding to?
  • What are the benefits? What specific value will the project create, and for whom?
  • What will it cost? In money, time, and people's attention — all three matter.
  • Is it worth it? Do the expected benefits justify the cost and risk?
Use this

Business case template — the simple version.

Six rows is enough for most small-organisation projects. Fill these in honestly and the decision usually writes itself.

Element What to write Example — online donation platform
Problem What issue are we solving? Donors drop off at our current donation page — 40% abandon before completing.
Goal What do we want to achieve, and by when? Reduce abandonment to under 15% within six months of launch.
Benefits What value will it create? Estimated €18k additional annual donations; better donor experience.
Costs Money, time, resources required. €3,500 platform setup; 6 weeks; 2 staff, 1 external developer.
Risks What could go wrong? Integration with existing CRM; donor confusion during cutover.
Decision Go / No Go — and why. Go — benefits outweigh costs in year one.

Alignment

Does this project support your organisation?

"Alignment" sounds abstract, but it's a simple test: if someone senior asked "why are we doing this project rather than something else?" — could you give a clear answer?

For small businesses

Projects should serve one of three commercial goals.

  • GrowthNew customers, new markets, new revenue streams.
  • EfficiencyLower costs, better margins, faster delivery.
  • Customer valueBetter products, stronger retention, higher satisfaction.

For charities & non-profits

Projects should serve the mission, not drift away from it.

  • MissionDirect advancement of your stated charitable purpose.
  • Social impactMeasurable change in the lives of beneficiaries.
  • Community benefitLong-term value for the people and places you serve.

The quiet danger

"Mission drift" is the silent killer of charity effectiveness. Projects get approved because they're fundable or exciting — not because they advance the mission. Every new project should be justified against your strategic plan, not just your enthusiasm.

In Ireland

Strategic thinking matters for funding and support.

Irish small businesses and charities don't just need a strategy internally — they need to articulate it clearly when applying for any kind of external support. The main agencies each have their own expectations.

For small businesses

Local Enterprise Office (LEO)

LEOs provide grants, mentoring, and trading online vouchers for small businesses across Ireland. Every application requires a clear business plan showing strategic direction, not just a product idea.

For growth-stage companies

Enterprise Ireland

EI supports ambitious Irish companies looking to scale, export, or innovate. They expect a credible growth strategy, international market analysis, and evidence of commercial potential before committing funding.

For charities & community orgs

Pobal & the Charities Regulator

Pobal administers major government-funded programmes and expects measurable social impact. The Charities Regulator's Governance Code requires every registered charity to have a documented strategic plan.

The common thread: whatever support you're seeking, you'll need to show that your projects fit into a coherent, funded, measurable strategy — not just a list of good intentions.

Prioritisation

You can't do everything.

Even after you've filtered out the bad ideas, you'll still have more good ideas than you can resource. Prioritising isn't about choosing between good and bad — it's about choosing between good things.

The simplest useful framework is the value/effort matrix. Plot each candidate project on two axes — how much value it delivers, and how much effort it requires — and the priorities emerge.

Avoid this

Four strategic mistakes small organisations make.

These show up again and again — usually at the same organisations, year after year, because no-one steps back and asks why.

Starting projects without clear, measurable goals — "improve engagement" isn't a goal.
Running too many projects at once, so none of them get the focus they need.
Ignoring the full cost — especially the hidden staff-time cost, which is usually the biggest.
Never evaluating whether the benefits actually materialised after the project ended.
Key takeaway
Good projects start with clear purpose — not just good planning. A well-executed project on the wrong problem is still a failure.
