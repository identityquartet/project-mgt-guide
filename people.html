Topic 04

Projects succeed or fail because of people.

You don't need a big team — you need clarity, communication, and the right roles.

Even the best plan won't work if people aren't aligned.

Why it matters

Projects aren't just tasks. They're people working together.

Even the simplest project brings together different roles, different expectations, and different priorities. The hardest problem in most projects isn't technical — it's human.

Every project you run will involve people with:

  • Different roles — what they do and are accountable for
  • Different expectations — what they think success looks like
  • Different priorities — what matters most to them individually

Poor communication is consistently cited as one of the most common causes of project failure. Getting people aligned early — and keeping them aligned — matters more than any Gantt chart.

Academic foundation

Effective stakeholder management is critical to project success — organisations must understand and balance the interests of all groups affected by their activities.

— Freeman (1984), Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach
Roles

Who does what?

Every project — however small — needs three roles covered. Same person can wear multiple hats, but the hats themselves must exist.

Project Lead

The person who holds it all together.

  • Responsible for coordination
  • Keeps the project on track
  • Decides when to escalate
  • Owns the outcome

Team Members

The people doing the actual work.

  • Complete the tasks
  • Deliver the outputs
  • Flag blockers early
  • Contribute expertise

Stakeholders

Everyone else with something at stake.

  • Affected by the outcome
  • May influence decisions
  • Need to be kept informed
  • Funders, board, beneficiaries

Reality for small orgs

In a small business or charity, one person often holds two or three of these roles at once. That's fine — as long as the roles are named, the person knows which hat they're wearing when, and someone else can see the full picture.

Stakeholders

Not everyone needs the same level of attention.

Stakeholders are anyone affected by your project — directly or indirectly. The trick is to work out how much time each one genuinely needs, then spend it there rather than everywhere.

A simple 2×2 matrix — often called the Power/Interest Grid or Mendelow's Matrix — sorts stakeholders into four groups based on how much influence they have over your project and how interested they are in it.

Communication

Most project problems come from poor communication.

Not budget. Not scope. Not technical complexity. The same three words keep coming back: unclear, late, or missing communication.

Good communication looks like

  • Clear updates — short, specific, and honest about progress.
  • Regular check-ins — predictable rhythm beats sporadic deep-dives.
  • No surprises — bad news early is worth more than good news late.
  • Two-way listening — ask questions, don't just broadcast.

A simple rhythm

Example schedule

  • Weekly Team check-in (15–30 min, same time each week)
  • Fortnightly Update to close stakeholders (one email)
  • Monthly Board / funder report (progress + risks + asks)
  • Quarterly Review and reset
Leadership

You don't need to be a manager to lead a project.

Leadership on a small-team or volunteer project isn't about authority. It's about showing up with clarity and supporting the people around you.

  • Be clear. Say what you mean, confirm what you heard, put it in writing when it matters.
  • Be consistent. Your team needs to know what to expect from you — show up the same way each week.
  • Support your team. Remove blockers, spot burnout, say thank you more often than you think you need to.
  • Solve problems early. Small issues raised on week two are easier to fix than a crisis at month three.
In practice

Working with volunteers and limited resources.

Managing people in a charity or community organisation in Ireland comes with a different set of realities. You can't manage volunteers the way you'd manage a paid team — motivation, not authority, is what gets work done.

Reality 01

Most people aren't paid

Volunteers are giving you their spare time. Every hour you ask for is an hour away from family, work, or rest — act accordingly.

Reality 02

Availability shifts

People's lives change. Build projects with buffer, cross-train where you can, and make roles documented enough that someone else could step in.

Reality 03

Motivation beats control

People stay because the work matters to them. Keep the mission visible, celebrate progress publicly, and share the "why" behind every ask.

In Ireland, organisations like Volunteer Ireland and The Wheel (the national representative body for charities and community organisations) offer free governance and people-management resources tailored to the Irish context — worth bookmarking if you manage volunteers regularly.

Avoid this

The four people-mistakes that keep coming back.

If a project is struggling, the cause is usually one of these four — and usually more than one at once.

Unclear roles — no-one's sure who owns what, so things fall between the cracks.
Poor communication — updates are too rare, too long, or too vague to act on.
Ignoring stakeholders — someone important finds out too late and blocks progress.
Assumed understanding — everyone thinks they agreed, but nobody wrote it down.
Key takeaway
Clear roles and good communication matter more than perfect plans — the human side is where most projects are actually won or lost.
