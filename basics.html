Topic 01

What is project management — and why does it matter?

A simple explanation for small businesses and charities. No jargon, no frameworks, no MBA-speak.

Just what you actually need to know.

Start here

A project is not just "work".

A project is a specific kind of work with three defining features. If all three apply, you're running a project — even if it doesn't feel like one.

Temporary It has a defined beginning and end — not something that runs forever.
Has a clear goal You know what "done" looks like before you start — a specific outcome.
Produces something new A result, product, or change that didn't exist before — not routine, repeated work.

Examples of projects

Launching a new website · Running a community event · Improving an internal process · Applying for a grant · Opening a new location · Rolling out a new policy.

Core idea

Project management is how you get things done.

In one sentence: project management is the set of habits and tools that help a group of people turn an idea into a finished result, on time and on budget.

It's really about four things, in order:

  • Planning — deciding what needs to happen and when.
  • Organising — getting the right people and resources in place.
  • Delivering — actually doing the work and keeping it on track.
  • Finishing — closing it out properly and learning for next time.

The formal definition

Project management involves applying knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet the project requirements.

— Project Management Institute (2021), PMBOK Guide
Why it matters

Without it, things fall apart.

Every small organisation has tried to run a project without any structure at some point. It rarely ends well. Here's the contrast.

Without project management

  • Missed deadlines and moving goalposts
  • Unclear roles — "I thought you were doing that"
  • Wasted time, money, and goodwill
  • Poor results — or no result at all

With project management

  • A clear structure everyone can see and follow
  • Better outcomes — you actually finish what you start
  • Less stress — fewer late-night scrambles
  • Confidence — you know what's coming next
Important difference

Not everything is a project.

Many tasks feel project-like but are actually just ongoing operations. Knowing the difference helps you decide which approach to use.

Project

Finite work with a goal and an end date.

  • TemporaryClear start, clear finish.
  • UniqueA specific one-off outcome.
  • Has an endDelivered, then closed.
vs

Daily work

Ongoing operations — the steady stream.

  • OngoingNo defined end.
  • RepetitiveSimilar tasks week to week.
  • ContinuousKeeps the organisation running.

A simple test

If you can describe what "finished" looks like, and that finish has a date — it's probably a project. If the work just continues indefinitely, it's daily operations.

How projects work

Every project follows a simple lifecycle.

Whether it takes a week or a year, whether it's a school fundraiser or a government contract, every project moves through the same five stages.

01

Start

The idea. Who's it for, and why now?

02

Plan

Map out the work, time, and resources.

03

Do

The team delivers the actual work.

04

Monitor

Track progress, adjust as you go.

05

Finish

Close out, review, and move on.

In practice

A real example: running a charity fun-run.

Here's what the lifecycle looks like when a local Irish charity decides to organise a summer fundraiser for the community food bank.

Stage 01 · Start

The idea takes shape

The committee decides to run a 5K fun-run this June with a fundraising target of €5,000 for the local food bank. They identify the lead organiser and agree the scope before anyone starts booking anything.

Stage 02 · Plan

Turning the idea into a plan

Book the park, line up sponsors for t-shirts and water, set the registration fee, map the route, plan volunteer shifts, and confirm insurance. Draft a simple budget and a risk list (weather being the obvious one).

Stage 03 · Do

The day itself

Volunteers arrive at 7am to set up. Registration opens at 8am. The run starts at 9am. Marshals are positioned along the route. A small team handles photos, refreshments, and the finish line.

Stage 04 · Monitor

Staying on top of the day

Track registrations against target, check volunteer cover at each station, watch the weather, handle any first-aid incidents. Adjust on the fly — reassign a marshal, move a refreshment table, bring the finish line forward if needed.

Stage 05 · Finish

Closing it out properly

Count the final total raised. Thank donors, sponsors, and volunteers personally. Publish the result. Hold a short debrief: what worked, what didn't, what to change next year. Archive the documents so whoever runs it next has a head start.

Key takeaway
Project management isn't complicated. It's just clarity and structure — knowing what you're doing, who's doing it, and when it's done.
