Home / Topics / Sustainability

Build projects that create lasting value.

Good projects don't just deliver results — they consider long-term impact. Sustainability isn't just environmental; it's about smarter, more responsible decisions at every step.

Thoughtful today. Worthwhile tomorrow.

Why it matters

Short-term thinking leads to long-term problems.

It's easy to optimise for the project's launch date and forget what happens next month, next year, or next funding cycle. The consequences quietly compound over time.

Projects that ignore sustainability tend to:

  • Waste time, money, and physical resources
  • Create hidden ongoing costs long after the launch
  • Damage reputation and trust with stakeholders
  • Deliver short-lived results that need redoing within months

The academic view

Sustainable project management means considering environmental, social, and economic impacts across the full lifecycle — not just at delivery. It represents a meaningful evolution beyond traditional project management.

— Silvius & Schipper (2014), Sustainability in Project Management Competencies
Core idea

It's really about making better decisions.

A "sustainable project" doesn't need a green certificate, a sustainability officer, or complicated metrics. It needs a handful of sensible habits, applied consistently.

  • Uses resources efficiently — no more hours, money, or materials than genuinely needed.
  • Avoids unnecessary waste — of effort, attention, paper, power, and goodwill.
  • Creates long-term value — the benefit continues after the launch party ends.
  • Considers people and impact — staff, volunteers, beneficiaries, neighbours.
Think in three areas

Sustainability isn't just environmental.

The widely-used "triple bottom line" framework breaks sustainability into three dimensions. All three matter; good projects consider all three from the start.

Environmental

Protecting the planet, however small your footprint.

  • Reduce waste wherever practical
  • Minimise energy use in offices and events
  • Prefer digital over printed materials
  • Choose reusable over single-use

Social

Supporting people — inside and outside the project.

  • Support staff, volunteers, and the community
  • Treat all stakeholders fairly and respectfully
  • Create positive, measurable impact
  • Be transparent about what you're doing and why

Economic

Using money and resources wisely — for lasting value.

  • Use resources wisely, not extravagantly
  • Avoid unnecessary costs and complexity
  • Focus spend on what creates long-term value
  • Plan for the ongoing cost, not just the launch
In practice

Small changes make a big difference.

None of these require extra budget or formal policies. They just require a moment's thought at the start of the project.

  • Use online tools instead of printed materials — shared documents, digital registrations, email updates. Cheaper, faster, and easier to update.
  • Choose local suppliers — lower delivery emissions, stronger community ties, and money stays in the local economy.
  • Avoid over-complicated solutions — if a spreadsheet does the job, don't build a database. Simpler solutions last longer.
  • Build something that lasts — choose durable tools, document what you do, and make it easy for someone else to maintain after you.
Use this

A five-question sustainability check.

Before you kick off any new project, take two minutes to sit with these questions. If the honest answer to most of them is "no" or "not sure", pause and rethink.

Ask yourself — before starting

  1. Does this project create long-term value, or just a short-term win?
  2. Are we using resources efficiently — time, money, materials, attention?
  3. Can we reduce waste at any stage — before, during, or after?
  4. Is this genuinely beneficial for all our key stakeholders?
  5. Are we thinking beyond the launch — about what happens six months later?

In Ireland

Why this matters for your organisation.

Sustainability isn't just a principle for large corporations. For Irish small businesses and charities, it shapes everything from cost structure to funding eligibility.

For small businesses

Cost efficiency compounds

Every unnecessary expense, every wasted material, every pointless subscription erodes your margin. Sustainable choices — local suppliers, digital workflows, durable tools — usually save money too.

For charities

Impact & responsibility

Charities are held to a higher standard — rightly so. Stakeholders expect you to spend every euro as if it were your own, and to make decisions that reflect your values, not just your budget.

For both

Funding depends on it

Irish and EU funders — Pobal, Enterprise Ireland, EU programmes — increasingly require evidence of sustainable practice. Accountability and impact measurement are becoming standard expectations, not bonuses.

Avoid this

Four habits to notice — and stop.

Most sustainability failures aren't dramatic. They're small daily habits that add up over a project's life.

Focusing only on short-term results — launch day matters, but so does month three, month twelve, year three.
Over-using resources "just in case" — over-printing, over-ordering, over-engineering. The waste adds up.
Ignoring long-term impact — on staff, beneficiaries, community, environment, reputation.
Treating sustainability as optional or a "nice to have" — reserved for when there's budget for it.
Key takeaway
Sustainable projects aren't more complex. They're simply more thoughtful — made with the next year in mind, not just the next week.
